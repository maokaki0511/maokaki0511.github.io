SAP Wallet

An event check-in and agenda system designed for an internal conference, connecting online interactions with offline event-day operations.

Take a Glance

From a single check-in feature to a complete event-day experience.

SAP Wallet was created for a large internal conference with around 3,000 attendees. Instead of treating check-in as an isolated function, I reframed the experience around the attendee’s real journey: browsing the agenda, finding the right session, arriving at the venue, completing check-in, and collecting participation records.

My Role UX/UI Designer responsible for user flow, interface design, and prototype iteration.
Team & Duration 4-person team, completed within around 5 weeks.
Context Internal conference tool supporting event-day operation and attendee engagement.
Tools Figma, prototyping, design system adaptation, cross-functional communication.

Challenge

The original request was about check-in, but the real problem was the full event journey.

How might we

help attendees understand where to go, what to attend, and how to complete check-ins smoothly during a large internal event?

Final Solution

I moved the experience from function-centered to journey-centered.

1

Agenda-first homepage

The homepage was structured around the event agenda, because attendees naturally needed to know which session to attend before thinking about check-in.

2

Check-in inside session detail

Instead of placing check-in as a disconnected entry point, the action was moved closer to the session detail flow, making it easier to understand when and where to check in.

3

Participation badges

I designed participation records as collectible badges by session type, balancing user engagement with development feasibility.

4

Operationally practical UI

The interface was designed to support the real event-day flow, where users needed quick recognition, clear next steps, and low-friction interactions.

Discovery

The key insight came from looking at what attendees would actually do on the event day.

Initial assumption

At the beginning, the project was framed mainly as a check-in system. This made the check-in button feel like the center of the product.

Reframed understanding

In the real scenario, attendees would not start from “I want to check in.” They would first need to browse the agenda, choose a session, locate the right place, and then complete check-in at the appropriate moment.

Check-in was not the starting point

The first user need was orientation: knowing what was happening, where to go, and what to do next.

02

Design Process

I clarified the flow, adjusted the information architecture, and iterated the interface around real event behavior.

Step 01

Map the event-day journey

I started by thinking through the attendee’s path before, during, and after each session. This helped reveal that the product should guide users through the event instead of only providing check-in access.

Step 02

Restructure the homepage

The homepage was reorganized around the agenda, so users could first understand the event structure and then enter each session detail page naturally.

Step 03

Place check-in in context

I placed the check-in action closer to the session detail flow, reducing the gap between “what I want to attend” and “what I need to do now.”

Step 04

Design participation badges

Instead of creating overly detailed individual records for every poster or activity, I designed badges around session types to create a collectible experience while keeping the scope practical.

Takeaways

This project changed how I think about designing for offline-online workflows.

Design beyond isolated functions

A feature may look clear on its own, but the real user experience depends on where it appears in the journey. In this project, check-in only made sense when placed inside the attendee’s event-day context.

Understand the physical scenario behind the interface

For products connected to offline operations, interface design has to consider movement, timing, location, and the user’s attention under real conditions.

Balance engagement with feasibility

Participation badges improved motivation, but the design also had to respect development time and operational complexity. A good solution is not only engaging, but also realistic to build and maintain.
